Motion Design for the launch of the magazine Branding Fiction. The project started with a simple question: why do some names and logos feel like they’ve always been here, even when they never were? We call them fictional brands, and they’re more than an uncanny twinge: they borrow the grammar of real branding so well our brains fill in the shelf space. Inside the book, Lorenzo Bernini maps how these invented labels work: the cues that trigger recognition, the ways they become folklore, and what they reveal about culture. This is achieved by analysing the most iconic fictional brands across film, TV, games, and the internet, allowing you to trace their origins, visual language, and brand impact. The motions has been used for the Social launch and the Event visuals

00Magazine. 00 is a new beginning, a new starting point that sees widespread commitment, collective awareness, and welfare policies as the tools to prevent the unthinkable from happening. In each issue, 00Magazine focuses on a specific crisis of global significance, providing a pathway that takes the reader through the various stages of the crisis, starting from its origin to its overcoming. Issue 00 “Nuclear Attack” offers an examination not only of the actions taken by government agencies, but also of the active involvement of citizens, scientists, and associations that work or have worked toward a common goal: the suppression of nuclear risk.

Rhytuals is a bookazine created during a five days workshop held by Jeremy Leslie, founder of MagCulture. Rhytuals explores how rhythm structures social behavior and transforms collective actions into rituals. It examines rhythm’s role in shaping identity, community, and resistance, revealing its power as a unifying force across cultures and histories. The chapters of the magazine unfold simultaneously on the same pages. A non-conventional reading method that allows each page to be turned multiple times with completely different rhythms depending on the chapter. The issue 01 is about Political Activism.

What does your discomfort look like? text
In an age dominated by digital communication, memes have taken a central role in our daily interaction. They are not limited to being a hilarious pastime, but have become a veritable form of language. Born within the freedom of the web, memes have mutated into influential marketing and political propaganda tools, often in controversial ways. Memetic Warfare Vol.1 sets out to memetically represent the diverse and complex facets of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict without giving answers but, on the contrary, stimulating the emergence of new insights and debate: How do memes describe this conflict to us? What opinion do we have about it now? Who is the good guy, who is the bad guy?
